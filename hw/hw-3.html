Fishbone Analysis: IFSC 11003 Student Value

Problem Statement: This analysis examines the factors that influence whether a student gains the highest practical value‚Äîskills, portfolio development, and confidence‚Äîfrom IFSC 11003. A fishbone (cause-and-effect) diagram was used to identify and organize the primary contributing factors.

Analysis by Category

1. Student Background & Mindset

Student mindset strongly affects outcomes in IFSC 11003. Self-initiated learners tend to engage more deeply, practice independently, and recover more quickly from setbacks. Students who are willing to experiment and break things develop resilience and improve debugging skills.

A desire to create visually polished work increases motivation, especially during CSS and the final project, while intellectual curiosity encourages exploration beyond required examples. These traits support persistence and deeper understanding.

2. Time, Schedule, & Life Constraints

Many students balance coursework with jobs and family responsibilities. Busy schedules reduce available practice time. Students who do not participate in real time often struggle more than those who attend live sessions because they miss immediate feedback and live problem-solving.

Technology access matters as well. A lack of a reliable laptop, unstable internet, or limited screen space increases friction and frustration, particularly for remote participants.

3. Technical Setup & Tools

Cloud-based development environments reduce setup barriers, but students still struggle with file management and naming conventions. Poor organization frequently creates errors that are difficult for beginners to diagnose.

Students often resist or misunderstand version control, initially viewing GitHub as unnecessary or intimidating. This is compounded by terminal anxiety and the cognitive load of working across multiple tools.

4. Course Structure & Instruction

The course is intentionally cumulative, making early assignments foundational for later work. When students skip or delay early tasks, gaps compound quickly. The fast-paced design further limits recovery time, and relying on recordings can create a false sense of progress.

Assessments are skill-based and low-stakes, encouraging experimentation, but they rely on consistent participation. Feedback limitations due to large enrollment can also allow misunderstandings to persist longer than ideal.

5. Study Habits & Practice Behaviors

Students who live-code during class and debug outside of class consistently show stronger skill development. Debugging reinforces cause-and-effect understanding in code.

Students who experiment beyond provided examples and maintain consistent weekly practice retain skills more effectively and adapt more easily as complexity increases.

6. Support Systems & Resources

Weekly labs provide structured practice and early intervention opportunities. Video and written tutorials support different learning styles and allow students to review material at their own pace.

Real-time Q&A and instructor willingness to re-explain concepts reduce intimidation and encourage persistence, supporting student confidence and retention.

Excluded Factors

Age, raw intelligence, and tool quality were considered but excluded as primary causes. Success in IFSC 11003 depends more on engagement, practice, and support than on innate ability or demographics.

Suggestions for Improvement

  1. Emphasize file management and naming conventions earlier in the course.
  2. Communicate the cumulative nature of assignments more explicitly.
  3. Add early low-stakes checkpoints to identify struggling students sooner.
  4. Continue to normalize debugging and failure as part of the learning process.
  5. Encourage real-time participation whenever possible.

AI: What is gained and what might be lost when a team relies too heavily on predefined categories instead of creating problem-specific ones?

Student: If teams always use the same predefined categories without customization, they tend to generate more generic responses and solutions that may not be well suited to the specific problem.

Question 5

AI: What criteria would you use to decide when it is worth creating custom categories?

Student: Meaningful customization requires domain expertise within the group so that categories can be created thoughtfully and accurately.

Question 6

AI: What risks arise if a team without sufficient domain expertise builds a fishbone diagram anyway?

Student: The value lies in the discovery process itself. If the diagram is used constructively to broaden perspective rather than to produce definitive conclusions, it can still be useful even without deep expertise.

AI: How would you explain the purpose of a fishbone diagram so teams do not mistake it for proof of root cause?

Student: I would explain that the process of building the diagram is an opportunity for different perspectives to examine the same problem. The outcome may not be prescriptive, but it can be informative and helpful in identifying next steps.

AI: Where does a fishbone diagram fit best in a larger problem-solving process?

B) Process Reflection

Using an AI to provide Socratic questioning on fishbone diagrams was more effective than I initially expected. Instead of receiving a static explanation of what a fishbone diagram is, the question-and-answer format forced me to articulate why the technique works, when it is useful, and where it fits in a broader problem-solving process. The pacing of one question at a time was especially helpful, as it prevented me from jumping ahead to conclusions and instead encouraged incremental refinement of my thinking.

What stood out most was how the Socratic approach surfaced assumptions I didn‚Äôt initially realize I was making, particularly around the role of domain expertise and the risk of treating the diagram as a definitive diagnosis rather than a discovery tool. Being prompted to explain limitations as well as strengths helped deepen my understanding of fishbone diagrams as a process rather than an artifact. The experience felt closer to a guided discussion than to traditional AI-generated content, which made the learning more active and reflective.

In terms of prompting strategy, explicitly requesting Socratic questioning with one question at a time was critical and is something I would not change. That instruction clearly shaped the interaction and kept the AI from reverting to lecture-style explanations. If I were to adjust anything in the future, I might specify the desired length or complexity of each question depending on the depth of analysis I want, or ask for occasional reflective pauses to synthesize learning mid-exercise rather than only at the end.

Overall, this approach was effective in stretching my thinking and helped me engage more deeply with the topic than a conventional explanation would have. It reinforced that how you prompt an AI matters as much as what you ask it to explain.
